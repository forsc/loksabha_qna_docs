                                    TRIBAL AFFAIRS






                                    LOK SABHA
                                





UNSTARRED 


                                    QUESTION NO:
                                

5086






                                    ANSWERED ON: 
                                

04.04.2022






Tribal Population




Naba (Hira) Kumar Sarania














                        Will the Minister of





TRIBAL AFFAIRS


                        be pleased to state:-















                                                    (a) the total population of the tribals in the country, the State/district/ name-wise details thereof;(b) the types in which languages of the Scheduled Tribes has been divided on the basis of the region, the details thereof;(c) whether there are many tribal languages which are still not understood by the other people of the country; (d) the types of languages spoken by the tribes in the country which are endangered and the details thereof; and(e) the details of the language spoken by different tribes in the North Eastern States along with the tribes with which these languages are associated with?
                                                





ANSWER




                                                        (a): The population of  Scheduled Tribes (STs)  in the country, as per Census 2011  is 10.45 crore. State / district wise population of Scheduled Tribes as per Census 2011 in India is given at Annexure I. Scheduled Tribe community wise population for States /districts in India is available at the link ( https://censusindia.gov.in/2011census/PCA/ST.html ) of the website of Office of the Registrar General & Census Commissioner, India. (b): As informed by Central Institute of Indian Languages, Department of Higher Education, Ministry of Education that languages spoken by the Scheduled Tribes (STs) are considered ‘tribal languages’. However, the tribal languages can be categorized according to language family-wise, i.e. Indo-Aryan, Tibeto Burman, Austro-Asiatic, Dravidian & Andamanese and then State/UT-wise. (c): As per Central Institute of Indian Languages, Department of Higher Education, Ministry of Education, there is no information available in this regard. (d):  As informed by Central Institute of Indian Languages, Department of Higher Education, Ministry of Education, there are several tribal languages that are endangered. Ministry of Education, Government of India has initiated a Scheme known as “Scheme for Protection and Preservation of endangered languages of India” (SPPEL). Under this scheme, the Central Institute of Indian Languages (CIIL), Mysore works on endangered languages. In the first Phase, 117  tribal languages are identified to work on. Details of the languages are given at Annexure II.  (e):  Central Institute of Indian Languages, Department of Higher Education, Ministry of Education informed that the tribes of the North Eastern States use their mother tongues as home languages. However, being used within small clans, groups or communities, their mother tongues are restricted in small spaces. In Assam, Bodo, Mishing, Karbi, Rabha, Dimasa, Tiwa, Deori, etc, tribes use their languages among them, whereas they shift to other regional languages in interlingual situations. In Arunachal Pradesh Nyishi, Apatani, Monpa, Millang, Koro, Wancho, etc. are used intralingually but use Hindi for interlingual communications. In Nagaland, Konyak, Ao, Lotha, Angami, Chokri, Sangtam, Zeme, Zemi, Yimkhiungrü, Chang, Khiamniungan, Rengma, Phom, etc. are used intralingually; whereas, Nagamese is used in interlingual situations. In Manipur, different Manipuri, Kuki (Zomi group of languages) and Naga languages (Liangmai, Thangkhul, Maram, Mao, Rongmei, etc.) are used. In Mizoram, there are so many tribal languages that are used as home or local languages; whereas, Mizo and English have been used as their lingua francas. In Tripura, Kokborok and its varieties, a few Kuki languages are used. As dominated by Bengali, it is used as the major lingua franca of the state. In Meghalaya, speaking tribes of Khasian languages/varieties such as Khasi, Pnar, War, Maram, Lyngam, etc., mostly use Khasi; the Bodo-Garo group of tribes use Garo and Bodo; whereas, English is also used interlingually. In Sikkim, many tribes like Lepcha, Limbu, Bhotia, Rai, Ta-mang, Newar, etc., use Nepali interlingually.
